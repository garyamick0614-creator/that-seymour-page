This week

Storms hit, cleanup's on, and there's still plenty going on around town.

Updated April 29, 2026.

Storm recovery

April 27 storms tore through Jackson County.

A line of thunderstorms came across central Indiana on Sunday afternoon — tornadoes, hail, hard wind, and flooding. The Weather Service confirmed a tornado on the ground in Jackson County between Bobtown and Cortland, just northwest of town. A lot of folks have trees down and yards to clear. Lucky timing in one way: the storm hit during Make Seymour Shine week (Apr 13–17), so the city's extra curbside pickup was already running.

Around town

  • Crossroads Acoustic Festival — Apr 24–25 (recap on Seymour Main Street)
  • City Council — met Apr 27. Next ones: May 11 and May 26, 7 PM at George Green Chambers
  • Crossroads of Cultures — May 2 downtown
  • School budget re-vote — May 1 (the first one failed Apr 17, 614 to 924)
  • BurgerFest 2026 — September 19, downtown. 36th year. Elexco sponsoring the main stage again.

Seymour stories

The town has more story than people give it credit for.

A few things every Seymour visitor should know — from BurgerFest on Chestnut St to a WWII airfield outside town. Photos are public domain.

🎸

"Small Town" was about right here

John Mellencamp grew up on Bruce Drive. Some of the lyrics in "Small Town" came straight out of growing up in Seymour. Downtown is the same Chestnut & 2nd intersection in the photo — just a little less worn.
✈

Freeman Field — the airfield that changed integration

Five miles outside town. Built in 1942, hosted the Tuskegee-trained 477th Bombardment Group, and the 1945 "Freeman Field Mutiny" pushed the Army a little closer to integrated officers' clubs.
🍔

BurgerFest — 36 years of the world's largest hamburger parade

Every September. Bun Run, eating contest, ketchup slide, live music until 11. Started in 1990 to claim the "Home of the Hamburger" title; the proceeds bankroll the local scholarship. Saved a couple kids' college runs over the years.
🏛

Why "Crossroads of America"

Seymour sits where the B&O Railroad crossed the Jeffersonville & Indianapolis line in the 1850s. That junction is the reason there's a town here at all. Brownstown (county seat, photo) handles the courthouse end of things.
